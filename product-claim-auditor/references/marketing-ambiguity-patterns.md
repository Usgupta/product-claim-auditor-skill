# Marketing Ambiguity Patterns

Use this reference to identify where tech product marketing often hides buyer-relevant truth.

## "Up To" Claims

Pattern: A large number is made legally safe by "up to".

Research questions:
- What is the median or typical improvement?
- Which workload, benchmark, mode, ambient condition, and configuration produced the maximum?
- Does the user's intended task match that workload?
- Is the improvement versus the previous generation, an older model, or a cherry-picked baseline?

## Imaginary Spec

Pattern: A page combines the best number from one variant with the lowest price from another variant.

Research questions:
- Can the advertised price, performance, range, storage, display, battery, and accessory bundle be bought together?
- Which trim or SKU gets each headline number?
- Does the configurator preserve the claim at checkout?
- Are required options excluded from the starting price?

## Configuration-Dependent Specs

Pattern: A claim is true only with specific wheels, tires, charger, memory tier, storage tier, cooling system, display mode, subscription, or accessory.

Research questions:
- Which configuration changes the result?
- Is the required configuration default, optional, or unavailable in the buyer's region?
- Does choosing the advertised benefit reduce another important benefit?

## Invented Or Rebranded Specs

Pattern: A branded term replaces a standard comparable term.

Examples:
- "Unified memory" may still be a fixed shared RAM pool.
- "Motion rate" may not equal panel refresh rate.
- "QLED", "ULED", or "QNED" may still be LCD rather than OLED.
- "1-inch type" camera sensors are not literally one inch wide or diagonal.
- "1.5K" displays may not have a 1500-pixel dimension.

Research questions:
- What is the standard industry term?
- What physical measurement or standard test does the branded term map to?
- Does the term obscure a lower quantity, weaker component, or incompatibility?

## Software Feature Padding

Pattern: Launch events use software features to make a new hardware product seem more differentiated than it is.

Research questions:
- Is the feature exclusive to the new product?
- Is it from a third party such as Google, Microsoft, Adobe, or OpenAI?
- Is it also available on older models or competitors?
- Does it require cloud processing, a subscription, login, region support, or future update?

## Cherry-Picked Baselines

Pattern: A new product is compared to a much older model to produce a large multiplier.

Research questions:
- How does it compare to the immediately previous generation?
- How does it compare to discounted last-generation stock?
- What is the real-world difference in the buyer's workload?
- Is the comparison against a worn, base, thermally constrained, or non-equivalent model?

## Tradeoff Claims

Pattern: Marketing implies two benefits arrive together even when they trade off.

Examples:
- Faster performance and longer battery life may not occur simultaneously under full load.
- More shatter resistance can mean less scratch resistance, and vice versa.
- Higher zoom magnification can mean more cropping, not better detail.

Research questions:
- Can both benefits be realized at the same time?
- What setting, mode, or condition produces each number?
- Does improving one metric degrade another metric the user cares about?

## Removed Low-End Option Framed As Upgrade

Pattern: The base model gains storage, memory, or another spec while the lower-priced option disappears.

Research questions:
- Did the entry price increase?
- Is the "upgrade" optional or forced?
- What was the previous lowest total price for the product family?
- Is the buyer paying more for capacity they may not need?

## Premium Material Language

Pattern: Common materials are described with prestigious but weakly specific labels.

Examples:
- "Aerospace grade aluminum"
- "Surgical grade stainless steel"
- "Military grade toughness"

Research questions:
- What exact alloy, grade, treatment, or standard is used?
- Is there a named certification or only a descriptive phrase?
- Does the material improve durability, weight, repairability, corrosion resistance, or only marketing perception?

## Pointless Or Misleading Measurement Choice

Pattern: The company chooses the measurement that gives the biggest-looking number, not the most buyer-relevant one.

Examples:
- Thinnest point instead of maximum thickness including camera bump.
- Peak brightness instead of typical full-screen brightness.
- Megapixels instead of sensor size and image quality.
- Maximum digital zoom instead of optical reach and resolved detail.

Research questions:
- What measurement determines everyday usability?
- Is the headline metric peak, partial-screen, lab-only, or sustained?
- What do independent tests show under normal use?

## Demo And Sample Media Caveats

Pattern: "Shot on" or demo claims omit professional production support.

Research questions:
- Was extra lighting, stabilization, external lenses, filters, rigs, drones, microphones, or post-production used?
- Were stock or licensed images used?
- Were default device features disabled to make external hardware work?
- Can a normal buyer reproduce the result with only the product?

## Final Classification

Classify each audited claim as one of:

- Buyer-relevant and supported
- True but configuration-dependent
- True but not exclusive
- True but misleading measurement
- True but weakly useful
- Unsupported by available evidence
- False or contradicted
- Unclear; requires vendor clarification
